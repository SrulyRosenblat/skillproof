Subject: Kioku Tea Society, a monthly subscription that ships one single-origin Japanese green tea sourced directly from a named family farm in Uji, Kyoto, along with a brewing card handwritten by that season's farmer.
Audience: People in their late 20s to 40s who default to coffee out of habit, have started noticing specialty coffee and natural wine, and are looking for a slower, more deliberate ritual rather than another caffeine source.
Primary job: Convince a curious first-time visitor that this is a real, traceable relationship with a specific farm rather than "tea in the mail," so they commit to their first box.

## Why these choices

The brief only said "subscription, people get something recurring." Left at
that level, the page would end up describing a shape (a box, a monthly
charge, a cancel-anytime line) that fits literally any subscription business.
Naming the actual product — single-origin Japanese green tea, tied to one
named farm per shipment, with a brewing card from the farmer who grew it —
gives every section something concrete to be about: whose hands picked the
leaves, what farm is currently featured, how the tea should actually be
brewed. The audience is coffee-habituated rather than "tea people," because
that's the harder, more specific conversion problem, and it's the one that
makes "why should I care" copy meaningful instead of generic. The page's one
job is to make the sourcing feel real and specific, not to recite features,
because the risk for a mail-order tea subscription is reading like the fifty
other boxes-of-stuff services.
