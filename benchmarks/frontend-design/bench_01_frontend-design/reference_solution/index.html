Every month we ship a single-origin green tea from one named family farm in Uji, Kyoto — plus a brewing card written by the person who grew the leaves.

What actually shows up at your door

Most tea subscriptions are a sampler: five foil packets, a printed insert, a QR code to a page you'll never visit. Kioku ships one tea at a time, in a quantity meant to be brewed daily for a month, from one farm that we name outright. You'll know it's the Hayashi family's first spring flush, not "premium sencha, origin: Japan."

For people who switched to good coffee, and are ready to switch again

This isn't written for people who already keep a gaiwan on the counter. It's for the reader who upgraded their coffee order two years ago — grinds beans at home now, knows their roaster's name — and has started to feel like coffee is doing a job rather than being a ritual. If you've noticed yourself wanting something slower in the morning, and you're the kind of person who reads the label on a good bottle of olive oil, this is built for you.

Why one farm, not a rotating sampler

A single farm means a single point of accountability. When the Hayashis have a difficult harvest, we say so on the brewing card instead of quietly swapping in filler leaves. When you brew it wrong the first time, the card tells you the water temperature that farm actually uses, not a generic 175°F that applies to no tea in particular. Over a year of boxes, you build a map of specific hillsides in Kyoto, not a vague sense that you "like sencha."

  • We call the farm before each shipment and ask what's actually ready to ship this month.
  • The farmer writes the brewing card themselves — temperature, steep time, and one line about the season.
  • We ship within a week of that call, so the leaves haven't been sitting in a warehouse.

What it costs to keep this going

A month of Kioku is $34, shipped free within Japan and at cost everywhere else. You can pause before any shipment — we'll ask the farm to hold your leaves rather than charge you for tea that sits in transit. Cancel any time; there's no contract, because a farm relationship isn't something you should have to be locked into.
